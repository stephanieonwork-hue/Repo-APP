03 How It Works

The idea is straightforward: we look at what happens in markets with OOH versus a market without it, and measure how each changes over time. Because we’re tracking the same markets before and after the campaign, we can strip away noise like seasonality, promotions, or other media — and isolate what OOH alone is contributing to SKIP’s results.

This approach is called Difference-in-Differences (DiD), and it’s the same method leading brands across CPG, retail, and QSR use to make confident media investment decisions.

04 Why Prairie Markets (Not Toronto vs. Vancouver)

The quality of the answer depends entirely on the quality of the comparison. For SKIP to trust the results, the markets we compare need to be moving in the same direction before the campaign starts. Otherwise, any post-campaign difference could just be noise — and SKIP would be making decisions on unreliable data.

FAIL  Toronto vs. Vancouver
Trends diverge before the campaign → invalid comparison
PASS  Prairie Markets (Recommended)
Parallel pre-trends → post-campaign gap = true OOH effect
Why this matters: If the markets aren’t in sync before the campaign, any difference we see after could just be a continuation of what was already happening — not the effect of OOH. Matching markets upfront is what makes the results trustworthy.

What Happens When Parallel Trends Fail

Bottom Line

Calgary, Winnipeg, Regina, and Edmonton share similar demographics, economics, weather patterns, and media environments. This means they naturally trend together — so when we see a difference after OOH launches, we can be confident it’s the OOH driving it.

There are alternative statistical approaches for comparing different-sized markets, but they add complexity and are less precise. Starting with well-matched markets gives us the cleanest, most reliable read.

05 Case Study: This Approach Already Works

QSR Media Channel Incrementality Test

This isn’t theoretical. A national QSR brand faced the same question SKIP is asking today: Is this media channel actually driving business, or does it just look like it is? We used the exact same methodology to give them a definitive answer.

How we set it up: 200 matched store pairs (with and without the media channel), selected based on similar demographics, sales history, and competitive environment. 18 months of baseline data to confirm the markets were trending together before the test.
